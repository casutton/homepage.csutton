Machine Learning Specialism FAQ

Here is some informal advice from me (Charles Sutton) about the Machine Learning specialism, which is part of the MSc programme in the School of Informatics, University of Edinburgh.

What classes should I take?

The specialism web site gives some advice on course selection. Following this advice is a good way to get a sensible selection of courses. However, you should only pick courses for which you have the pre-requisites, and there can be good reasons to consider options outside the specialism table. The courses listed in the specialism are advice, but they are not strict requirements. The strict requirements that your courses need to satisfy are the DPT rules for your degree.

In Semester 1, MLPR and IAML cover some of the same material, so you shouldn't normally take both courses at the same time. IAML is primarily intended to prepare you to use a variety of machine learning methods, and spends time going through the application of some methods that MLPR will only mention. In contrast, MLPR spends more time on mathematical detail, and is intended for those who wish to be involved in core machine learning research in future. The MLPR website gives details of its mathematical pre-requisites.

If you would like to participate in the specialism, my personal advice is:

  1. If you are serious about machine learning, and have the background, take MLPR and PMR. If you don't take these courses, you will not be prepared to do a summer project in developing new ML methods.
  2. If you do not have the mathematical background for MLPR, take IAML instead, which would prepare you for more applied ML projects.
  3. PMR has similar mathematical requirements to MLPR, but can still be taken after IAML instead of MLPR. You can re-evaluate at the beginning of Semester 2 whether you should take PMR.
  4. MLP is a practical course that develops skills in implementing and running machine learning methods. It is set up as a series of projects rather than formal lectures, so it will not give you an overview of the field, and it does not replace either IAML or MPLR. If you decide to take MLP, you should take it in addition to one of IAML and MLPR.
  5. Consider taking DME if you would like to learn about examples of applying advanced machine learning methods to various problems.
  6. Reinforcement learning is another area of machine learning that has become increasingly active over the past few years, e.g., with high-profile successes at Google DeepMind.
  7. Randomness and Computation is a theory course (more mathematical than MLPR and PMR), which could be useful background for those interested in the theoretical end of machine learning.
  8. Also consider taking an outside course in statistics if you would like additional theoretical background that will help you in further study of machine learning, especially if you are considering a PhD someday. Please see the FAQ below about statistics courses.
  9. You can also consider taking courses in the databases specialism, as in your working life it will often help you to know the theory behind databases.
  10. The Extreme Computing course is also especially popular and relevant.
  11. Then fill out your programme with courses in other research areas that apply machine learning, such as natural language processing, speech processing, bioinformatics, computer vision, graphics, or robotics. The Social and Technical Networks course is another course that is relevant to a lot of the types of modern applications used in machine learning.

What programming background should I have?

You should be able to program in a language like Java, C++, Python, etc. If you do not have this background, you should take the Introduction to Java course.

How much maths do I need?

Some students find the maths used in PMR and MLPR to be a bit hard going. This seems to happen especially to students with a computer science background, because most of CS uses discrete maths, whereas machine learning tends to need continuous maths, especially calculus and linear algebra. For this reason, students with a physics or engineering background sometimes have less trouble.

Both the MLPR and PMR course pages describe the maths background we expect.

Can I take courses from outside Informatics?

Yes, you may wish to consider taking a course or two from the Statistics/OR programme because of the close relationships between statistics and ML. This is especially relevant if you are considering a PhD in machine learning someday.

If you wish to consider this, please see this page with general information about the statistics/OR programme. Also, you should read this timetable carefully because their programme is set up very differently than ours. For example, they have several 5-credit courses.

Based on their topics the following classes would be especially relevant:

  1. MATH11085 STh Statistical Theory
  2. MATH11006 MP Mathematical Programming
  3. MATH11029 SM Stochastic Modelling

The timetable for the OR part is weekly, and set up very differently from Informatics. You need to check the timetables carefully.

The OR courses have a cap on their class sizes because it is a very large programme. Before you take any OR courses, you need to contact the OR Programme Director (currently Dr Julian Hall < J.A.J.Hall @ ed.ac.uk >). Before you take any statistics courses, you need to contact the Statistics Programme Director (currently Dr Bruce Worton < b.worton @ ed.ac.uk >) to receive permission.

There is one unexpected interaction to keep in mind: If the total of your Levels 9-10 Informatics credits and your credits from outside Informatics adds up to more than 30 points, then you are in a special case where you need to talk to your Personal Tutor for advice. This is very important.

I can't fit 5 courses from the official list. Is that OK?

Yes, this is fine. The specialism guidelines are advisory and not strict requirements. If you are unsure about your program, email or speak to your personal tutor for advice.

If I do this specialism, will I get a job? Will it use machine learning?

I don't know. There are a couple reasons why I don't know. First, I don't know you, or how skilled you are. Second, and more fundamentally, as a lecturer, I know how to get a teaching job in academia—that's the job I have, after all. It has been many years since I tried to get a job in the computer industry.

Really I can say only two things: First, overall in Informatics, my impression is that our MSc graduates do reasonably well in the job market. Second, there are lots of companies in industry that use or want to use machine learning.

Does this page have a disclaimer?

In particular, I may not update this page every time the School regulations are updated (I'll try my best, though). Therefore, if you see an official School page that appears to conflict with this page, the official School page is probably right. In case of any doubt on regulations, please contact the ITO. If you wish to discuss your course selections, please talk to your Personal Tutor.
